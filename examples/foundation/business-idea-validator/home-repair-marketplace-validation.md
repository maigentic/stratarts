# Business Idea Validation Report

**Idea**: Home Repair Marketplace Platform
**Date**: 2025-10-29
**Analyst**: Claude (StratArray)

---

## Executive Summary

This mobile app aims to connect homeowners with vetted handymen/contractors for small home repairs (under $500), differentiating from Thumbtack and HomeAdvisor through curated matching (2-3 providers vs. 10-20), transparent pricing, and a contractor subscription model ($99/month unlimited leads). The founder is a product manager with 8 years at tech companies and 2 failed app launches, but no marketplace, home services, or two-sided platform experience. While the market is massive ($50-80B for small repairs), it's dominated by well-funded incumbents who have spent hundreds of millions building supply and demand.

**Recommendation**: NO GO
**Confidence Level**: High
**Overall Score**: 4.1 / 10

This is a classic "graveyard idea"—dozens of startups have attempted to disrupt Thumbtack/HomeAdvisor with better UX, lower fees, or niche positioning, and nearly all have failed. Two-sided marketplaces have brutal chicken-and-egg dynamics: you need contractors to attract homeowners, and homeowners to attract contractors. Without significant capital ($500K-$2M+) to subsidize both sides during the 18-36 month cold-start period, this will stall. The founder's proposed differentiation (curated matching, subscription model) has been tried and rejected by the market. Recommend abandoning this idea or pivoting to a dramatically different approach.

## Dimension 1: Problem-Solution Fit

**Score**: 6.5 / 10 (Weight: 25%)

The problem—homeowners struggle to find reliable, fairly-priced help for small repairs—is real but not as severe as the founder believes. Yes, Thumbtack and HomeAdvisor show 10-20 contractors, which creates comparison fatigue. Yes, there are scams and low-quality work. But these platforms have addressed the problem well enough that 70M+ homeowners use them annually. The market has spoken: the pain is mild enough that people tolerate the friction in exchange for choice and competition.

Problem frequency is moderate. Homeowners need small repairs 3-6 times per year, which creates recurring demand. However, this isn't daily or weekly—it's occasional. Unlike SaaS tools used every workday or fitness studios visited 3x per week, home repair platforms have low engagement. Users might open the app twice per year, which makes building habits and retention extremely difficult.

Current workarounds are actually pretty good. Thumbtack works for most people—you post a job, get 3-10 bids, read reviews, pick someone. Yes, it's time-consuming (30-60 minutes to compare bids), but for a $300 repair, that's acceptable effort. HomeAdvisor has invested billions in vetting and insurance. Nextdoor provides trusted referrals from neighbors. TaskRabbit handles simpler tasks. The "gap" that a new entrant would fill is narrow—slightly better UX and slightly lower friction. That's not a 10x improvement; it's a 20% improvement. Insufficient to overcome switching costs.

The proposed solution—curated matching with 2-3 contractors instead of 10-20—sounds good in theory but has been tried and failed. Porch (raised $100M+) attempted this exact model and struggled. Why? Because homeowners don't trust the platform to curate well. They want to see options and make their own choice. Limiting to 2-3 providers feels restrictive, not helpful. If one of those 3 is booked or overpriced, the homeowner is back to square one. The "paradox of choice" applies to complex decisions (retirement plans, health insurance), not simple ones (which handyman should I hire?).

Transparent upfront pricing is also already solved. Many contractors on Thumbtack now post rates ($80/hour, $150 minimum, etc.). The subscription model for contractors ($99/month unlimited leads) might attract some providers, but it's risky. Contractors on Thumbtack complain about $20-40 per lead, but they only pay when they get a viable job. Asking them to pay $99/month with no guarantee of lead volume or quality is a hard sell. They'll demand to see hundreds of active homeowners in their area before subscribing—which you don't have yet (chicken-and-egg).

Willingness to pay is unvalidated. Friends saying "I'd use this" and 30 Facebook comments are not purchase commitments. Did anyone offer to pay $20 to join a waitlist? Did any contractors commit to the $99/month subscription? No. These are low-cost social signals, not real demand.

**Key Insight**: The problem is real but already addressed by well-funded incumbents. Your proposed improvements are incremental, not transformational, and have been tried (and mostly rejected) by the market.

**Evidence**:
- Problem Severity: 6/10 (frustrating but not critical; existing solutions work)
- Problem Frequency: 7/10 (3-6x per year, but low engagement)
- Current Workarounds: 7/10 (Thumbtack, HomeAdvisor, TaskRabbit are "good enough")
- Willingness to Pay: 5/10 (social interest only, no payment commitments)

## Dimension 2: Market Opportunity

**Score**: 5.8 / 10 (Weight: 20%)

The U.S. home improvement market is indeed $600B annually, but this is misleading. That figure includes major renovations ($50K+ kitchen remodels), new construction, and large contractor projects. The relevant market for sub-$500 repairs is smaller—estimated at $50-80B annually. Still large, but heavily fragmented and served by incumbents.

The TAM calculation is deceptive. Yes, 50M+ U.S. homeowners exist, and if each spent $500/year on small repairs via your platform at 10% take rate, that's a $2.5B TAM. But this assumes: (1) homeowners switch from existing solutions, (2) contractors join your platform, (3) you can compete with Thumbtack/HomeAdvisor's liquidity. All three are massive assumptions.

The SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market) is much smaller. Marketplaces are local—you need supply (contractors) and demand (homeowners) in the same geographic area. Let's say you launch in one mid-size city (population 500K). Perhaps 150K homeowners, 5% (7,500) might use your app annually, spending $400 average = $3M in transactions. At 10% take rate, that's $300K annual revenue for one city. To reach $10M revenue, you'd need to successfully launch in 30+ cities, each with localized contractor supply—an operational nightmare requiring $5M+ in funding and 3-5 years.

Market growth trajectory is flat to slightly declining. The "on-demand home services" wave peaked in 2014-2016 with Handy, Homejoy (shut down), Thumbtack, TaskRabbit, and dozens of others. Most failed or were acquired. The survivors (Thumbtack, HomeAdvisor) have consolidated the market. New entrants face entrenched competition and market saturation. The COVID-era home improvement boom (2020-2021) has cooled; spending is returning to pre-pandemic levels as interest rates rise and home sales slow.

Accessibility is extremely difficult. Two-sided marketplaces are the hardest business model to bootstrap. You need:
1. **Contractors first**: No homeowner will use an app with zero contractors available.
2. **But contractors won't join**: Without homeowner demand, why would they pay $99/month or even list for free?
3. **Chicken-and-egg problem**: You must subsidize one or both sides (free for contractors, pay homeowners to post jobs, etc.) until critical mass. This requires 18-36 months and $500K-$2M in runway.

The founder has $80K savings and could raise $150K from friends/family ($230K total). That's 6-12 months of runway with a small team, insufficient to solve the cold-start problem in even one city.

Monetization clarity is mixed. The proposed model: 10% transaction fee from homeowners + $99/month contractor subscriptions. Problems:
- **10% take rate**: Lower than Thumbtack (15-20% via lead fees) but you have no leverage. Contractors will demand lower fees or just stay on Thumbtack where homeowners already are.
- **$99/month subscriptions**: Unproven. Contractors are skeptical of subscription models without guaranteed lead volume. They prefer pay-per-lead (only pay for viable jobs) even if more expensive per lead.
- **Low AOV (Average Order Value)**: At $250 average repair, 10% = $25 per transaction. You'd need 400 transactions/month ($100K GMV) just to make $10K in revenue. Achieving 400 transactions/month in one city requires thousands of active users, which takes years to build.

**TAM Estimate**:
- Conservative: $50M (one city, 10% market penetration, $10M GMV × 10% take rate × 50 cities)
- Aggressive: $2.5B (national scale, 30% market penetration, but unrealistic without $100M+ funding)

**Key Insight**: The market is large but dominated by incumbents with network effects. Reaching meaningful revenue requires multi-city expansion and millions in capital to solve chicken-and-egg dynamics.

**Evidence**:
- Market Size Indicators: 7/10 (large market, but saturated and fragmented)
- Growth Trajectory: 4/10 (flat to declining; home services wave peaked 2014-2016)
- Accessibility: 3/10 (two-sided marketplace cold-start requires massive capital)
- Monetization Clarity: 6/10 (model is clear but unit economics are tough)

## Dimension 3: Competitive Advantage

**Score**: 2.5 / 10 (Weight: 20%)

The proposed differentiation—curated matching (2-3 providers), transparent pricing, contractor subscriptions, focus on sub-$500 repairs—is weak and has been tried by competitors with far more resources.

**Curated matching (2-3 providers vs. 10-20)**: This is not an advantage; it's a constraint. Homeowners want options. Limiting them to 2-3 feels like the platform is making the decision for them, which erodes trust. Porch tried this exact approach and struggled because homeowners abandoned the platform when the "curated" options didn't fit (booked, expensive, or poor reviews). Thumbtack's model works because it provides liquidity—if one contractor doesn't respond, there are 9 others. Your model breaks if 1 of your 3 curated contractors is unavailable.

**Transparent upfront pricing**: Already solved. Thumbtack and TaskRabbit both allow contractors to post rates. This isn't a differentiator.

**Contractor subscription model ($99/month)**: This has been attempted multiple times and generally fails. Why?
- **Contractors are risk-averse**: They prefer pay-per-lead (variable cost tied to actual jobs) over fixed monthly fees with uncertain lead volume.
- **Trust issue**: "Will I get $99 worth of leads this month?" Without proven lead flow, contractors won't commit.
- **Adverse selection**: The contractors willing to pay upfront are often desperate for work (lower quality), while top contractors stay with pay-per-lead platforms where they control how much they spend.

Bark (UK marketplace) tried subscription model for service providers and had to pivot back to pay-per-lead because adoption was too low.

**Focus on sub-$500 repairs**: This is niche positioning, but it's not defensible. TaskRabbit already serves this market for simpler tasks. Thumbtack handles jobs of all sizes. There's no technical or operational reason why Thumbtack couldn't optimize for small repairs if that segment proved lucrative. You're not creating a new market—you're segmenting an existing one, which only works if the segment is underserved (it's not) or you have an unfair advantage in serving it (you don't).

**Defensibility** is near zero. Marketplaces have only one moat: **network effects** (liquidity). More contractors attract more homeowners; more homeowners attract more contractors. This compounds over time—Thumbtack has 10M+ jobs posted annually and 300K+ contractors. A new entrant with 50 contractors and 100 homeowners in one city has no liquidity and no moat. Homeowners will use Thumbtack because there's more supply; contractors will use Thumbtack because there's more demand. Breaking this cycle requires massive marketing spend to subsidize both sides until you hit critical mass (typically 10-20% of incumbent's liquidity in a geographic area).

**Unfair advantages**: None. The founder has no:
- **Supply advantage**: No relationships with contractors, no exclusive partnerships
- **Demand advantage**: No audience of homeowners, no distribution channel
- **Technology advantage**: Marketplace matching algorithms are well-understood; no proprietary tech
- **Brand advantage**: Unknown brand vs. Thumbtack's $500M+ spent building trust
- **Capital advantage**: $230K total vs. competitors who've raised $500M-$1B+

**Time to market** is moderate (6-9 months to build app, onboard contractors, launch in one city), but speed doesn't matter when you're competing against entrenched platforms. Being first is valuable; being 10th with less capital is not.

**Moat Assessment**: No moat. Pure execution battle against better-funded, higher-liquidity incumbents.

**Key Insight**: Your competitive position is extremely weak. You're fighting network-effect businesses with zero liquidity, no capital advantage, and incremental improvements that the market has already rejected.

**Evidence**:
- Differentiation: 2/10 (features have been tried and failed by competitors)
- Defensibility: 1/10 (no moat; network effects favor incumbents)
- Unfair Advantage: 2/10 (no supply, demand, tech, or brand advantage)
- Time to Market: 6/10 (can build quickly, but irrelevant against incumbents)

## Dimension 5: Execution Feasibility

**Score**: 3.9 / 10 (Weight: 15%)

Technical complexity is moderate. Building a marketplace app (iOS, Android, backend) with contractor profiles, job posting, messaging, payments, and reviews is well-trodden territory. The founder could hire a dev team or use no-code tools (Bubble, Adalo) to build an MVP in 3-6 months for $30K-$60K. The technical lift is manageable.

However, **operational complexity is extreme**. Marketplaces aren't software problems—they're network-building and operations problems:

1. **Contractor onboarding**: Manually recruit 50-100 contractors in your launch city. This requires sales calls, in-person meetings, convincing them to try a platform with zero homeowner demand. Expect 5-10% conversion (you'll need to contact 500-1,000 contractors). Timeline: 3-6 months. Cost: $20K-$40K in sales/BD time or outsourced recruiting.

2. **Contractor vetting**: Background checks ($30-50 per contractor), license verification, insurance verification, reference checks. Liability if a contractor causes property damage or injury—you need robust screening and $2M+ liability insurance. Cost: $10K-$20K setup + $50K-$100K annual insurance.

3. **Homeowner acquisition**: Paid ads (Google, Facebook, Instagram) to drive job posts. CAC for two-sided marketplaces is $50-$200 per activated user (someone who posts a job and hires). If you need 500 active homeowners to provide enough demand for 50 contractors, that's $25K-$100K in acquisition costs. And churn is high—if only 20% post a second job, you're constantly refilling the top of the funnel.

4. **Liquidity management**: You must maintain 15-25 active contractors per job category (plumbing, electrical, handyman, drywall, etc.) in each city. If a homeowner posts a plumbing job and only 1 plumber is on the platform (and they're booked), the homeowner churns and never returns. This requires deep supply in every category before you're viable, which takes 12-18 months and significant capital.

5. **Quality control**: Disputes are inevitable (contractor no-shows, poor work, payment disputes). You need customer support (full-time hire or outsourced, $3K-$5K/month) and a dispute resolution process. One viral complaint ("This platform connected me with an unlicensed contractor who flooded my basement") can kill the brand.

**Capital requirements** are high relative to available funds. Breakdown:
- App development: $30K-$60K (MVP)
- Contractor recruitment: $20K-$40K (sales/BD)
- Homeowner acquisition: $50K-$100K (6 months of ads)
- Contractor vetting + insurance: $60K-$120K
- Operations (support, legal, admin): $20K-$40K
- **Total Year 1**: $180K-$360K

The founder has $230K total, leaving zero buffer for iteration, unexpected costs, or runway extension. Most marketplaces require 18-36 months to reach sustainability ($50K+ monthly GMV, 20% take rate = $10K/month revenue barely covering costs). At $230K, the founder has 9-12 months maximum, insufficient to solve the chicken-and-egg problem.

**Regulatory risk** is moderate to high. Operating a marketplace connecting homeowners with contractors creates liability exposure:
- **Contractor licensing**: Some states require platform verification of contractor licenses.
- **Worker classification**: Are contractors truly independent, or are they employees? Misclassification lawsuits (see Uber, Lyft) are expensive.
- **Liability for damages**: If a contractor causes property damage or injury, homeowners may sue the platform. Robust insurance and waiver language are essential.
- **Payment processing**: Requires PCI compliance, escrow for holding payments, fraud prevention.

**Distribution clarity** is very poor. How will the first 1,000 homeowners find this app?
- **Paid ads**: Expensive ($50-$200 per activated user), unsustainable without significant capital.
- **SEO**: Takes 12-18 months to build organic traffic; too slow for a bootstrapped marketplace.
- **Word-of-mouth**: Only works after you've delivered great experiences to 100+ customers; need initial traction first.
- **Partnerships**: Could partner with real estate agents, property managers, or home inspectors for referrals, but these take months to establish.

The most likely outcome: the founder spends $150K on development and initial marketing, acquires 50 contractors and 200 homeowners, realizes there's insufficient liquidity (homeowners churn because not enough available contractors; contractors churn because not enough jobs), and shuts down after 12 months. This is the graveyard of failed marketplaces.

**Risk Assessment**:
- **Technical risk**: Low (app development is straightforward)
- **Regulatory risk**: Moderate-High (contractor licensing, liability, worker classification)
- **Market risk**: Very High (dominated by well-funded incumbents with network effects)
- **Distribution risk**: Very High (chicken-and-egg problem, insufficient capital to solve)
- **Operations risk**: Very High (vetting, quality control, supply management)

**Key Insight**: Execution is high-risk—operational complexity is extreme, capital is insufficient to solve cold-start dynamics, and distribution has no clear path. This will likely fail within 12 months.

**Evidence**:
- Technical Complexity: 7/10 (app is straightforward)
- Capital Requirements: 2/10 ($230K is 30-50% of what's needed for marketplace cold-start)
- Regulatory Risk: 5/10 (moderate liability and compliance burden)
- Distribution Clarity: 2/10 (no viable path to acquire supply and demand simultaneously)

This idea should be abandoned. Two-sided marketplaces are the hardest business model to execute, and this one faces insurmountable challenges:

Homeowners will use the platform with more contractors; contractors will use the platform with more homeowners. You can't break this cycle without $1M-$5M to subsidize both sides for 18-36 months.

2. **Unvalidated differentiation**: Your proposed improvements (curated matching, subscription pricing) have been tried by better-funded competitors and rejected by the market. The market has spoken—homeowners want choice, contractors want pay-per-lead. You're not offering a 10x improvement; you're offering a lateral move.

3. **Insufficient capital**: $230K is enough to build an app and run limited marketing. It's not enough to solve chicken-and-egg dynamics, recruit contractors in multiple cities, subsidize early users, or sustain 18+ months to reach break-even. You'll run out of money before achieving critical mass.

4. **Weak founder-market fit**: You have PM skills but no marketplace expertise, no home services experience, and no network access to contractors or homeowners. Your 2 previous failed startups suggest you haven't yet found your product-market fit strengths. Attempting an even harder model increases failure risk.

**Core issues identified**:
- No moat or unfair advantage against billion-dollar incumbents
- Chicken-and-egg dynamics require $1M-$5M+ in capital to solve
- Differentiation is incremental and has been rejected by the market
- Founder lacks marketplace-specific skills and network access
- High operational complexity (vetting, liability, quality control, city-by-city expansion)

**Why this idea fails repeatedly**:
Dozens of startups have attempted to "fix" Thumbtack/HomeAdvisor with better UX, lower fees, or niche positioning:
- **Homejoy** (Y Combinator, raised $40M): Shut down in 2015 after quality control and contractor classification lawsuits
- **Handy** (raised $110M): Struggled with unit economics, sold to ANGI (HomeAdvisor parent) in 2018
- **Porch** (raised $100M+): Pivoted away from marketplace to software for contractors
- **Takl** (raised $27M): Shut down in 2022 after failing to compete with TaskRabbit and Thumbtack

The pattern: marketplaces are "winner-take-most" markets. The platform with the most liquidity wins. New entrants without significant capital to achieve liquidity faster than incumbents can grow inevitably fail.

**Alternative ideas to consider**:

### Pivot 1: Vertical SaaS for Contractors (Not a Marketplace)
Instead of building a two-sided platform, build software FOR contractors to manage their businesses:
- Scheduling, invoicing, payment processing, customer communication
- Similar to Jobber, Housecall Pro, or ServiceTitan (all $100M+ revenue companies)
- Monetize via SaaS subscriptions ($50-200/month per contractor)
- **Why this is better**: No chicken-and-egg problem, clear value prop, proven market, aligns with your PM/product skills

### Pivot 2: Neighborhood-Specific Concierge Service (Not a Platform)
Instead of a scalable app, offer a high-touch concierge service in one affluent neighborhood:
- Homeowners pay $50/month for unlimited "handyman coordination" (you find contractors, schedule, manage projects)
- You maintain relationships with 5-10 trusted contractors, negotiate bulk rates
- Essentially a "household manager" subscription
- **Why this is better**: No tech required, can start with 20-30 homeowners ($12K-$18K annual revenue), no cold-start problem, can charge premium for convenience

### Pivot 3: Exit Home Services Entirely
Given 2 previous failed apps and weak founder-market fit for marketplaces, consider:
- Focus on your PM strengths in a domain where you have expertise or network access
- Join a growing startup as a PM to learn more about what makes products succeed before founding again
- Consult as a fractional PM while exploring ideas in domains you understand deeply

**If you insist on pursuing this marketplace idea** (strongly discouraged):
1. Raise $1M-$3M before building anything (prove to investors that you understand marketplace dynamics and have a unique wedge)
2. Find a co-founder with deep home services or marketplace experience (ideally someone who's scaled supply at Thumbtack, TaskRabbit, or similar)
3. Identify a genuinely underserved niche (not "sub-$500 repairs"—that's served by TaskRabbit and Thumbtack) where incumbents have structural disadvantages
4. Develop an unfair advantage (exclusive supply, owned distribution channel, proprietary tech that improves matching 10x)
5. Plan for 3-5 year timeline to profitability with 18-36 months of capital to solve cold-start

Without these conditions, this idea will burn your $230K and 12-18 months with near-zero chance of success.

## Validation Experiments (Next 30-60 Days)

**IMPORTANT NOTE**: These experiments are designed to **disprove** the idea quickly before you invest significant capital. If 3+ experiments show negative signals, that's clear data to pivot.

### Experiment 1: Contractor Subscription Willingness Test

- **Objective**: Validate that contractors will pay $99/month for unlimited leads
- **Method**: Recruit 20 local contractors (handymen, electricians, plumbers) via Craigslist, Facebook groups, or in-person at Home Depot. Pitch the subscription model: "Pay $99/month, get unlimited homeowner leads in your area, no bidding wars, no per-lead fees." Ask: "Would you commit to 3 months ($297) to try this if I launch in 90 days?" Track how many say yes with a deposit.
- **Success Criteria**:
  - 50%+ (10+) commit with $50-100 deposit (validates contractor willingness)
  - Contractors confirm they'd actually use the platform if homeowner volume is there
  - Identify what lead volume they'd need ($99/month = ~5-10 jobs/month minimum to break even vs. Thumbtack's pay-per-lead)
- **Timeline**: 21 days
- **Cost**: $200 (incentives for contractor time)
- **Expected outcome**: NEGATIVE. Most contractors will say "Show me the homeowners first" or "I'll try it for free, but I'm not paying upfront."

### Experiment 2: Curated Matching Preference Test

- **Objective**: Validate that homeowners prefer 2-3 curated matches over 10-20 options
- **Method**: Survey 50 homeowners (friends, local Facebook groups, neighbors) who've used Thumbtack/HomeAdvisor. Present two scenarios: (A) "Platform shows you 10-15 contractors, you read reviews and compare," vs. (B) "Platform shows you 2-3 best-fit contractors based on your job, you pick one." Ask: "Which do you prefer? Why?"
- **Success Criteria**:
  - 70%+ prefer curated matching (validates core differentiation)
  - Qualitative feedback confirms they trust the platform to curate well
  - They'd be willing to use a new platform for this feature alone
- **Timeline**: 14 days
- **Cost**: $100 (survey incentives)
- **Expected outcome**: NEGATIVE. Most will prefer more options ("What if the 2-3 you show me are all booked or expensive?")

### Experiment 3: Manual Concierge MVP (No App)

- **Objective**: Test if homeowners will pay for your service without building anything
- **Method**: Offer a manual concierge service to 10 friends/neighbors: "Text me when you need a handyman. I'll find 2-3 vetted contractors, get quotes, schedule, and manage the job. You pay the contractor directly + $25 service fee." Deliver this manually (you call contractors, coordinate, etc.). Track: conversion rate, willingness to pay, repeat usage.
- **Success Criteria**:
  - 50%+ of 10 people use the service (5+ jobs completed)
  - They pay the $25 fee without hesitation
  - They say "I'd use this regularly if it was an app"
- **Timeline**: 30 days
- **Cost**: $0 (time only)
- **Expected outcome**: MIXED. Some will use it, but $25 fee may feel too high for the convenience, and manual coordination is exhausting (you'll realize why marketplaces need tech + scale).

### Experiment 4: Competitive Analysis Deep-Dive

- **Objective**: Understand why similar startups failed and what incumbents do well
- **Method**: Research 5-10 failed home services marketplaces (Homejoy, Takl, Handy's struggles). Read post-mortems, founder interviews, Crunchbase data. Analyze Thumbtack and TaskRabbit's product features, pricing, contractor reviews. Identify: What did failed startups get wrong? What moats do incumbents have? What gaps (if any) remain?
- **Success Criteria**:
  - Identify at least 3 structural reasons why competitors failed that you can avoid
  - Find at least 1 underserved niche that incumbents ignore
  - Validate that your differentiation is genuinely novel (not already attempted)
- **Timeline**: 14 days
- **Cost**: $0 (research only)
- **Expected outcome**: NEGATIVE. You'll discover that your proposed features have been tried and failed, and incumbents have no glaring gaps.

### Experiment 5: Capital Requirements Reality Check

- **Objective**: Validate how much capital you actually need to reach sustainability
- **Method**: Build a detailed financial model: (1) Contractor recruitment costs (assume $50-$100 per recruited contractor, need 50-100 per city), (2) Homeowner acquisition costs ($50-$200 per activated user, need 500-1,000 per city), (3) Monthly burn (ops, support, marketing, tech), (4) Revenue per transaction (10% of $250 avg repair = $25), (5) Transactions needed to break even. Calculate: How much capital to reach break-even in 1 city? How many cities to reach $1M revenue?
- **Success Criteria**:
  - Break-even is achievable within 18 months with <$500K capital
  - Path to $1M revenue doesn't require $5M+ and 50+ cities
  - Unit economics work (LTV > 3x CAC)
- **Timeline**: 7 days
- **Cost**: $0 (Excel model)
- **Expected outcome**: NEGATIVE. You'll discover you need $1M-$3M to solve cold-start in even one city, and $5M-$10M to reach meaningful scale.
